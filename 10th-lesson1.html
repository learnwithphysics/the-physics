📖 Introduction

Light helps us see objects around us. Whenever light falls on a shiny surface like a mirror, it bounces back. This process is called reflection. Curved mirrors produce different kinds of images depending on the position of the object. Understanding reflection helps us explain the working of mirrors used in vehicles, hospitals, telescopes and many optical instruments.

🎯 Learning Outcomes

  • Understand reflection of light.
  • Learn about curved mirrors.
  • Identify concave and convex mirrors.
  • Understand image formation.
  • Perform virtual experiments.

💡 What is Light?

Light is a form of energy that enables us to see objects. It travels in straight lines and can be reflected, refracted or absorbed depending on the material it strikes.

Examples

  • ☀ Sunlight
  • 💡 Electric bulb
  • 🔦 Torch
  • 🕯 Candle

🪞 Reflection of Light

Reflection is the bouncing back of light when it strikes a smooth surface. The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection. This principle is used in mirrors and many optical devices.

🪞 Curved Mirrors

A curved mirror is a mirror whose reflecting surface is curved. There are two types of curved mirrors.

Types of Curved Mirrors

Mirror Shape Main Use
Concave Mirror Curves inward Dentist mirror, Headlights, Solar furnace
Convex Mirror Bulges outward Vehicle rear-view mirror

🔍 Concave Mirror

A concave mirror has its reflecting surface curved inward. It can produce real or virtual images depending on the object's position.

Applications

  • 🦷 Dentist's mirror
  • 🚗 Vehicle headlights
  • 🔦 Torch reflectors
  • ☀ Solar cooker

🚘 Convex Mirror

A convex mirror has its reflecting surface bulging outward. It always forms a virtual, erect and diminished image.

Applications

  • 🚘 Rear-view mirrors
  • 🏪 Security mirrors in shops
  • 🏥 Hospital corridors

📍 Important Parts of a Spherical Mirror

Term Meaning
P (Pole) Centre point of the mirror.
C (Centre of Curvature) Centre of the sphere from which the mirror is formed.
F (Principal Focus) Point where reflected rays meet (or appear to meet).
Principal Axis Straight line passing through P and C.

💡 Remember

  • ✅ Concave Mirror → Can form real and virtual images.
  • ✅ Convex Mirror → Always forms virtual, erect and diminished images.
  • ✅ Reflection follows the law: Angle of Incidence = Angle of Reflection.

🖼 Image Formation by Concave Mirror

The type of image formed depends on the position of the object.

Object Position Image Formed Nature
Beyond C Between C and F Real, Inverted, Diminished
At C At C Real, Inverted, Same Size
Between C and F Beyond C Real, Inverted, Enlarged
At F At Infinity Highly Enlarged
Between F and P Behind Mirror Virtual, Erect, Enlarged

🚘 Image Formation by Convex Mirror

A convex mirror always forms a virtual, erect and diminished image behind the mirror, irrespective of the object's position.

  • ✅ Image is always virtual.
  • ✅ Image is erect.
  • ✅ Image is smaller than the object.
  • ✅ Used as rear-view mirrors because they provide a wider field of view.

📐 Mirror Formula

Formula

1/f = 1/v + 1/u

where,

  • f = Focal Length
  • v = Image Distance
  • u = Object Distance

🔍 Magnification

Formula

m = h₂ / h₁ = -v / u

  • m = Magnification
  • h₂ = Height of Image
  • h₁ = Height of Object

📊 New Cartesian Sign Convention

Quantity Sign
Distances to the left of Pole Negative (-)
Distances to the right of Pole Positive (+)
Height above Principal Axis Positive (+)
Height below Principal Axis Negative (-)

⭐ Exam Tips

  • Remember the mirror formula.
  • Practice ray diagrams regularly.
  • Know the image position for different object positions.
  • Learn the sign convention carefully.
